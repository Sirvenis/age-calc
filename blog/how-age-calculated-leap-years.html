How Age Is Calculated: The Science Behind Leap Years

Published: April 2026 | Reading time: 6 min

Calculating age seems simple — just subtract your birth year from the current year, right? Not quite. Accurate age calculation requires accounting for months, days, leap years, and varying month lengths.

Basic Age Calculation

Age is the difference between two dates. But unlike simple subtraction, dates involve months of different lengths (28-31 days) and leap years that add an extra day to February.

How Leap Years Work

A leap year occurs every 4 years — except years divisible by 100, unless also divisible by 400. This means:

  • 2024: Leap year (divisible by 4)
  • 2100: NOT a leap year (divisible by 100 but not 400)
  • 2000: Leap year (divisible by 400)
  • 2026: Not a leap year

February 29 babies face a unique situation — they only have a "real" birthday every 4 years. Most legal systems consider March 1 as their birthday in non-leap years.

The Algorithm

A proper age calculator works like this:

  1. Start with the difference in years
  2. If the current month is before the birth month, subtract 1 year
  3. If the months are equal but the current day is before the birth day, subtract 1 year
  4. Calculate remaining months and days after adjusting

Why Months Are Tricky

"One month" isn't a fixed duration. January has 31 days, February has 28 or 29, and April has 30. If you're born on January 31, what's your age 1 month later? February 28 (or 29) — not March 3. A good age calculator handles these edge cases correctly.

Time Zones and Exact Time

For most purposes, dates are sufficient. But if you need exact age down to the hour, time zones matter. Someone born at 11 PM in Tokyo is technically a day older in UTC than someone born at 1 AM in New York.

Conclusion

Age calculation is surprisingly complex when done correctly. Leap years, varying month lengths, and edge cases like February 29 birthdays all require careful handling. Use a calculator to get accurate results every time.
